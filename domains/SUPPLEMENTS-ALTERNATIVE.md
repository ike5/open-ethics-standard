# Supplements & Alternative Medicine — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Supplements & Alternative Medicine (Dietary Supplements, Herbal Remedies, Alternative Therapies, Wellness Products)
**Version:** 2026.0.1
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to the supplements and alternative medicine industry. Supplements operate in a regulatory gray zone that enables widespread consumer deception, and unethical practices in this domain exploit health anxiety, circumvent safety oversight, and endanger patients who may forgo evidence-based treatment.

---

## Category 1: Claims & Marketing

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| AP-1.1 | **Disease Cure Claims Without FDA Approval** | Marketing supplements as treatments, cures, or preventatives for specific diseases without FDA-approved evidence | Illegal health claim; patient endangerment |
| AP-1.2 | **Structure-Function Claim Stretching** | Exaggerating legitimate structure-function claims to imply disease treatment through implied language, imagery, or context | Regulatory circumvention; consumer deception |
| AP-1.3 | **Testimonial-Based Marketing** | Building promotional campaigns around individual success stories without disclosing that results are atypical or anecdotal | Misleading representation; exploitation of hope |
| AP-1.4 | **Doctor Endorsement Purchase** | Paying or incentivizing licensed professionals to endorse products without disclosing the financial relationship | Conflicted authority; transparency violation |
| AP-1.5 | **Condition-Specific Fear Marketing** | Using fear-based messaging about specific health conditions to drive supplement purchases as implicit solutions | Exploiting health anxiety for profit |

## Category 2: Product Quality

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| AP-2.1 | **Adulteration With Undisclosed Pharmaceuticals** | Adding active pharmaceutical ingredients to supplements without labeling them, creating unregulated drug products | Consumer endangerment; illegal adulteration |
| AP-2.2 | **Ingredient Amount Discrepancy** | Formulating products with significantly less active ingredient than labeled, or failing to verify label claims through testing | Label fraud; consumer deception |
| AP-2.3 | **Contamination With Heavy Metals** | Distributing products contaminated with lead, arsenic, cadmium, or mercury without testing or disclosure | Toxic exposure; negligence |
| AP-2.4 | **Proprietary Blend Opacity** | Using proprietary blend designations to hide the specific quantities of individual ingredients, preventing informed consumer choice | Deliberate opacity; informed consent violation |
| AP-2.5 | **Expired Ingredient Reprocessing** | Reusing expired raw materials by relabeling or reblending into new production batches | Consumer safety violation; fraud |
| AP-2.6 | **Non-GMO/Organic Claim Falsification** | Labeling products as non-GMO or organic without verification or certification | Certification fraud; consumer deception |

## Category 3: Access & Regulation

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| AP-3.1 | **DSHEA Loophole Exploitation** | Structuring products, labeling, and marketing to exploit the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act's weaker pre-market requirements | Regulatory circumvention; public health risk |
| AP-3.2 | **Import Evasion of Testing** | Importing raw ingredients or finished products through channels that bypass FDA entry testing and inspection requirements | Evading safety oversight; import fraud |
| AP-3.3 | **Third-Party Certification Avoidance** | Declining independent testing or certification programs (NSF, USP, ConsumerLab) while implying equivalent quality standards | Unverified quality claims; consumer deception |
| AP-3.4 | **Adverse Event Reporting Failure** | Failing to collect, investigate, or submit serious adverse event reports to the FDA as required by law | Concealing safety signals; regulatory violation |
| AP-3.5 | **New Dietary Ingredient Notification Bypass** | Introducing new dietary ingredients without submitting the required safety notifications to the FDA | Bypassing pre-market safety review |

---

## Category 4: Pricing

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| AP-4.1 | **Markup on Inexpensive Ingredients** | Pricing supplements at extreme margins over raw ingredient cost while implying the price reflects research or pharmaceutical-grade quality | Price gouging; value misrepresentation |
| AP-4.2 | **Subscription Trap Auto-Renewal** | Designing subscription programs with difficult cancellation processes, hidden auto-renewal terms, or deliberate friction in opt-out flows | Predatory billing; consent manipulation |
| AP-4.3 | **Clinical Trial Cost Misrepresentation** | Claiming product pricing reflects clinical research investment when trials were minimal, uncontrolled, or unpublished | False justification for premium pricing |
| AP-4.4 | **Insurance Coverage Misinformation** | Stating or implying that supplements are covered by health insurance or HSA/FSA plans when they are typically not eligible | Deceptive financial guidance |
| AP-4.5 | **Dose Inflation for Revenue** | Recommending dosages significantly higher than established safe or effective levels to accelerate product consumption and repurchase | Consumer health risk for revenue |

---

## Category 5: Data

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| AP-5.1 | **Customer Health Data Sale** | Selling purchase histories, health conditions, and supplement usage data to third parties including marketers and data brokers without meaningful consent | Health privacy violation; data exploitation |
| AP-5.2 | **Symptom Checker Upselling** | Using online symptom assessment tools that invariably recommend company products regardless of symptoms presented | Manipulative health tool; conflict of interest |
| AP-5.3 | **Influencer Undisclosed Paid Promotion** | Compensating social media influencers to promote supplements without requiring FTC-compliant disclosure of the paid relationship | Undisclosed advertising; consumer deception |
| AP-5.4 | **Purchase Pattern Targeting for Vulnerable Conditions** | Identifying customers with chronic or serious health conditions through purchase patterns and targeting them with aggressive remarketing for unproven products | Exploiting vulnerable patients |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Substantiate all claims** — ensure marketing claims are supported by competent and reliable scientific evidence; cease unapproved disease claims immediately.
2. **Verify product quality** — implement third-party testing for all products; disclose full ingredient quantities including proprietary blends; reject adulterated or contaminated ingredients at intake.
3. **Comply with regulatory obligations** — submit required new dietary ingredient notifications; report adverse events within legal timelines; cease import channel practices designed to evade testing.
4. **Practice transparent pricing** — disclose subscription auto-renewal terms prominently; provide simple cancellation processes; justify pricing claims with disclosed evidence.
5. **Protect consumer data** — obtain explicit opt-in consent before sharing health-related data; prohibit targeting based on inferred health vulnerabilities; require influencer partnerships to include FTC-compliant disclosures.
