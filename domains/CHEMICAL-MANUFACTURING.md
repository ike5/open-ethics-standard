# Chemical Manufacturing & Processing — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Chemical Manufacturing & Processing (Petrochemicals, Specialty Chemicals, Agrochemicals, Consumer Chemical Products)
**Version:** 2026.0.1
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to the chemical manufacturing and processing industry. Chemical production carries inherent risks to workers, communities, and ecosystems, and unethical practices in this domain conceal those risks, resist accountability for harm, and prioritize continued production of hazardous substances over safer alternatives.

---

## Category 1: Safety

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| CM-1.1 | **Accident Concealment** | Suppressing or minimizing reports of chemical releases, fires, or explosions to avoid regulatory scrutiny, legal liability, or public alarm | Suppressing safety-critical information; endangering communities |
| CM-1.2 | **Near-Miss Non-Reporting** | Failing to document, investigate, or communicate near-miss events that could have resulted in catastrophic releases, depriving the organization of critical prevention data | Systemic risk blindness; prevention failure |
| CM-1.3 | **Community Notification Failure** | Failing to inform surrounding communities about chemical hazards, release events, or shelter-in-place requirements in a timely manner | Denying communities the right to protect themselves |
| CM-1.4 | **Safety Data Sheet Opacity** | Publishing incomplete, outdated, or misleading safety data sheets that understate hazards, omit constituents, or fail to reflect current toxicological knowledge | Undermining worker and responder safety; informed consent violation |

## Category 2: Environmental

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| CM-2.1 | **Toxic Release Underreporting** | Systematically underreporting quantities of toxic releases to the EPA Toxic Release Inventory or equivalent national reporting systems | Environmental harm concealment; regulatory violation |
| CM-2.2 | **Superfund Cleanup Delay** | Using litigation, corporate restructuring, or jurisdictional arguments to delay or reduce obligations for remediation of contaminated sites | Evading cleanup responsibility; community abandonment |
| CM-2.3 | **Groundwater Contamination Concealment** | Detecting but not disclosing chemical migration into groundwater supplies used for drinking, agriculture, or ecosystem support | Concealing public health threat; environmental justice violation |
| CM-2.4 | **Air Quality Monitoring Avoidance** | Locating fenceline monitors to avoid detection of emissions, disabling monitors during peak release periods, or failing to install required monitoring at all | Deliberate measurement manipulation; community exposure concealment |
| CM-2.5 | **Waste Disposal Through Loophole Classification** | Reclassifying hazardous waste as non-hazardous through treatment exemptions or dilution to avoid proper disposal requirements | Regulatory circumvention; environmental endangerment |

## Category 3: Labor

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| CM-3.1 | **Chemical Exposure Non-Dlosure** | Withholding information from workers about the specific chemicals they handle, exposure levels they experience, or health risks documented in internal studies | Worker endangerment; informed consent violation |
| CM-3.2 | **Protective Equipment Cost Shifting** | Requiring workers to purchase their own personal protective equipment, providing inadequate or expired equipment, or substituting cheaper but less effective alternatives | Shifting safety costs to lowest-level workers |
| CM-3.3 | **Reproductive Hazard Concealment** | Failing to warn workers of documented reproductive hazards including mutagens, teratogens, and endocrine disruptors present in their work environment | Reproductive health violation; deliberate risk concealment |
| CM-3.4 | **Cancer Cluster Non-Investigation** | Declining to investigate or acknowledge elevated cancer rates among workers or surrounding communities when internal data suggests facility-related causation | Health harm denial; accountability avoidance |
| CM-3.5 | **Contractor Labor Arbitrage** | Using contracted labor for the most hazardous operations to avoid direct employer liability, insurance costs, and long-term health monitoring obligations | Worker classification exploitation; risk shifting |

---

## Category 4: Product

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| CM-4.1 | **Forever Chemical Production Continuation** | Continuing to manufacture PFAS and other extremely persistent chemicals despite known bioaccumulation and health harms while lobbying against restrictions | Ongoing harm for profit; intergenerational contamination |
| CM-4.2 | **BPA Replacement With Equally Harmful Alternatives** | Substituting bisphenol-A with structurally similar bisphenol analogs (BPS, BPF, BPAF) that carry equivalent endocrine-disrupting properties while marketing products as BPA-free | Regrettable substitution; consumer deception |
| CM-4.3 | **Product Stewardship Program Greenwashing** | Operating product stewardship programs that lack measurable targets, external verification, or enforcement mechanisms while promoting them as evidence of responsibility | Greenwashing; accountability theater |
| CM-4.4 | **Restricted Substance Export to Unregulated Markets** | Exporting chemicals banned or restricted in producing nations to countries with weaker regulatory frameworks, continuing production revenue at the expense of unregulated populations | Regulatory arbitrage; exploitation of regulatory gaps |

---

## Category 5: Regulatory

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| CM-5.1 | **Lobbying Against Chemical Regulation** | Funding campaigns to weaken, delay, or repeal chemical safety legislation including TSCA reforms, state-level restrictions, and international conventions | Undermining democratic safety governance for profit |
| CM-5.2 | **Revolving Door With EPA** | Placing former industry executives in regulatory positions or hiring departing regulators directly into compliance-adjacent industry roles | Regulatory capture; conflict of interest |
| CM-5.3 | **Self-Reporting Exploitation** | Manipulating voluntary self-reporting and self-auditing programs by selectively reporting favorable data while concealing violations | Abuse of regulatory trust; accountability evasion |
| CM-5.4 | **Trade Secret Overclaiming to Avoid Disclosure** | Classifying chemical identities, formulations, or health studies as trade secrets to prevent mandatory disclosure to regulators, workers, or the public | Transparency obstruction; public health suppression |
| CM-5.5 | **Preemption Strategy** | Supporting federal legislation that preempts stronger state-level chemical regulations, eliminating more protective local standards | Undermining protective governance; regulatory race to bottom |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Practice radical safety transparency** — report all incidents and near-misses within 24 hours; maintain current and complete safety data sheets; notify affected communities of hazards proactively.
2. **Account for environmental harm** — report toxic releases accurately; commit to and fund remediation of contaminated sites without litigation-driven delay; install fenceline monitoring with community-accessible data.
3. **Protect workers fully** — disclose all chemical identities and exposure data; provide employer-funded protective equipment; investigate health clusters with independent epidemiological review.
4. **Reform product strategies** — phase out extremely persistent chemicals on published timelines; reject regrettable substitutions; cease export of restricted substances to unregulated markets.
5. **Engage regulators ethically** — disclose all lobbying expenditures; implement cooling-off periods for regulatory transitions; limit trade secret claims to genuinely proprietary formulations while disclosing health and safety data unconditionally.
