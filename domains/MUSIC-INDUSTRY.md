# Music Industry — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Music Industry (Labels, Streaming, Publishing)  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to the music industry, encompassing record labels, streaming platforms, music publishers, and related entities. The music industry has a long history of power asymmetry between creators and intermediaries, where artistic output is monetized while artists receive disproportionate returns. Ethical failures in this domain undermine creative livelihoods, distort cultural expression, and erode trust between musicians and the industry that depends on their work.

---

## Category 1: Artist Compensation

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MU-1.1 | **Unsustainable Streaming Royalties** | Paying per-stream rates that fall below subsistence levels, making it impossible for most recording artists to earn a living from streaming alone | Exploitative compensation; livelihood destruction |
| MU-1.2 | **Label Contract Opacity**        | Using complex, non-standardized recording contracts that obscure royalty calculations, recoupment terms, and rights assignments | Informed consent violation; contractual deception |
| MU-1.3 | **Perpetual Rights Retention**    | Retaining ownership of master recordings and compositions in perpetuity through contracts that never revert rights to the creator, even after full recoupment | Property appropriation; creator disenfranchisement |
| MU-1.4 | **Recoupment Accounting Manipulation** | Structuring recoupment accounting to delay or avoid royalty payments through inflated expense deductions, cross-collateralization across projects, and selective expense allocation | Financial manipulation; royalty withholding |
| MU-1.5 | **Cross-Collateralization Abuse** | Applying unrecouped balances from one album to withhold royalties earned from another, trapping artists in debt across their entire catalog | Debt entrapment; unfair accounting |

## Category 2: Streaming

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MU-2.1 | **Per-Stream Rate Opacity**       | Refusing to disclose or standardize per-stream payout rates, making it impossible for artists to predict or verify earnings from their work | Transparency violation; financial opacity |
| MU-2.2 | **Playlist Placement Payola**      | Accepting payment or exchanging favors for playlist placement, editorial positioning, or algorithmic boosting without disclosure to listeners | Undisclosed paid promotion; market manipulation |
| MU-2.3 | **Fan Data Hoarding**             | Collecting detailed listener data but restricting artist access to fan demographics, listening patterns, and geographic distribution needed for touring and promotion | Data gatekeeping; artist disadvantage |
| MU-2.4 | **Algorithmic Self-Preferencing** | Designing recommendation algorithms to disproportionately promote tracks from catalogs owned or controlled by the platform's parent company, suppressing independent music | Anti-competitive bias; market distortion |
| MU-2.5 | **Minimum Stream Thresholds**     | Withholding payment for streams that fall below arbitrary minimum thresholds, pocketing revenue from millions of micro-plays by smaller artists | Wage theft; small-artist exploitation |

## Category 3: Rights & Ownership

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MU-3.1 | **Work-for-Hire Rights Transfer** | Classifying creative contributions as work-for-hire to强制 transfer all ownership rights from songwriters and performers to labels or publishers | Creator rights appropriation; ownership stripping |
| MU-3.2 | **Sample Clearance Gatekeeping**   | Maintaining opaque, prohibitively expensive, and inconsistent sample clearance processes that favor established artists and labels while blocking independent creators | Access inequality; cultural expression suppression |
| MU-3.3 | **Public Domain Extension Lobbying** | Lobbying to extend copyright terms beyond reasonable limits to keep older works out of the public domain, restricting cultural reuse and derivative creation | Cultural enclosure; public domain theft |
| MU-3.4 | **Mechanical Rate Suppression**    | Using industry negotiation power, statutory rate freezes, or procedural delays to suppress mechanical royalty rates below fair market value | Rate manipulation; creator underpayment |
| MU-3.5 | **Reversion Right Obstruction**    | Employing legal and procedural barriers to prevent copyright reversion to creators after the statutory window, including contract clauses that waive reversion rights | Statutory rights obstruction; perpetual control |

## Category 4: Data

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MU-4.1 | **Stream Count Manipulation**     | Allowing or failing to detect inflated stream counts through bots, organized play farms, or internal manipulation that distorts royalty pools | Fraud; revenue diversion from legitimate artists |
| MU-4.2 | **Royalty Reporting Opacity**      | Providing incomplete, delayed, or unintelligible royalty statements that prevent artists from verifying whether they are being paid correctly | Financial transparency violation; audit obstruction |
| MU-4.3 | **Fan Data Platform Monopoly**    | Consolidating fan engagement data within proprietary platforms and refusing to provide artists with direct access to their own audience information | Anti-competitive data hoarding; artist dependency |
| MU-4.4 | **Listening Data Sale to Advertisers** | Selling granular listener behavioral data, mood profiles, and demographic information to advertisers without meaningful consent from the listeners whose data is monetized | Privacy violation; data exploitation |

## Category 5: Diversity & Access

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MU-5.1 | **Independent Artist Platform Disadvantage** | Designing platform features, analytics, and promotional tools that primarily benefit major-label content, creating a two-tiered system that marginalizes independent artists | Structural inequality; market access denial |
| MU-5.2 | **Catalog Consolidation**          | Acquiring and consolidating music catalogs to reduce diversity of ownership, concentrate market power, and diminish the variety of music available to listeners | Monopolistic concentration; cultural homogenization |
| MU-5.3 | **Regional Music Suppression**    | Deprioritizing or excluding non-Western, non-English-language, and regional music from recommendation systems and editorial curation | Cultural bias; geographic discrimination |
| MU-5.4 | **Algorithmic Bias in Recommendations** | Deploying recommendation algorithms that replicate existing popularity patterns, reinforcing the visibility of already-dominant artists while suppressing emerging and underrepresented creators | Systemic bias; opportunity hoarding |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Compensate artists fairly** — publish transparent per-stream rates; eliminate cross-collateralization across projects; ensure contracts clearly disclose all deductions; support royalty reversion after recoupment.
2. **Ensure streaming transparency** — disclose all paid playlist placements; provide artists direct access to fan data; prohibit algorithmic self-preferencing; eliminate minimum stream payment thresholds.
3. **Respect creator rights** — limit work-for-hire classification to genuinely commissioned works; streamline sample clearance; oppose further copyright term extension; support fair mechanical rates.
4. **Protect data integrity** — implement bot detection and stream validation; provide clear, timely royalty reports; share fan data with artists; obtain explicit consent before selling listener data.
5. **Promote diversity and access** — design platform features accessible to independent artists; support regional music visibility; audit recommendation algorithms for demographic bias; resist catalog consolidation that reduces market diversity.
