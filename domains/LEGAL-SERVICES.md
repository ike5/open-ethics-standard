# Domain: Legal Services

**Version:** 2026.0.1
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This standard defines ethical practices and violations within the legal services profession, encompassing billing integrity, access to justice, conflicts of interest, marketing honesty, and client data stewardship. It addresses systemic practices that erode public trust in legal institutions and create barriers to equitable legal representation. Legal practitioners and firms operating under this domain standard commit to transparent, fair, and client-centered conduct.

---

## Billing

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| LE-1.1 | Accurate time recording | Record billable hours with good-faith accuracy, reflecting actual time spent on client matters | Inflating billable hours by rounding up, recording overlapping time entries, or billing for work not performed |
| LE-1.2 | Billing rate transparency | Bill clients at rates commensurate with the experience level of the professional performing the work | Billing junior associate work at partner rates or assigning senior billing codes to work performed by less experienced staff |
| LE-1.3 | Task necessity justification | Perform only tasks that are reasonably necessary for the client's matter and disclose anticipated scope in advance | Padding bills with unnecessary research, redundant document review, or excessive meeting time to increase revenue |
| LE-1.4 | Fee arrangement clarity | Establish and communicate fee structures, billing increments, and expense policies before engagement | Imposing unexpected charges, vague expense line items, or retroactive fee adjustments without client consent |
| LE-1.5 | Alternative fee model availability | Offer alternative billing arrangements—flat fees, capped fees, or success-based structures—where appropriate | Refusing to offer any billing model other than hourly rates, even when flat or capped fees would better serve the client's interest |

## Access

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| LE-2.1 | Pro bono commitment | Dedicate a meaningful percentage of firm capacity to pro bono representation for underserved populations | Exploiting the justice gap by concentrating exclusively on high-margin matters while refusing pro bono or reduced-fee work |
| LE-2.2 | Sliding scale availability | Offer income-adjusted fee structures for clients of moderate means who do not qualify for free legal aid | Avoiding sliding scale arrangements that would make services accessible to middle-income individuals facing legal crises |
| LE-2.3 | Self-help resource clarity | Provide plain-language legal information and self-help resources that genuinely empower individuals to navigate legal processes | Creating deliberately complex self-help resources that drive individuals toward paid representation rather than enabling genuine self-advocacy |
| LE-2.4 | Geographic accessibility | Maintain office hours or remote consultation options that serve rural and underserved communities | Concentrating services exclusively in affluent urban centers with no remote or outreach options for distant clients |
| LE-2.5 | Language access | Provide interpretation services and multilingual materials to ensure non-English speakers can meaningfully access legal help | Failing to offer interpretation or translated materials, effectively excluding non-English-speaking individuals from legal representation |

## Conflicts of Interest

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| LE-3.1 | Dual representation prohibition | Avoid representing parties with adverse interests in related matters unless all parties provide informed written consent | Double-dipping by representing both sides of a transaction or opposing parties in related disputes without full disclosure and consent |
| LE-3.2 | Referral fee disclosure | Disclose all referral fees, kickbacks, and financial relationships with recommended service providers to clients | Concealing referral fee arrangements that create financial incentives to recommend specific vendors over the client's best interest |
| LE-3.3 | Class action settlement fairness | Advocate for settlement distributions that prioritize class member recovery over attorney fees and administrative costs | Skimming disproportionate attorney fees from class action settlements while class members receive minimal or symbolic compensation |
| LE-3.4 | Former client conflict screening | Screen all new matters against former client records to identify and address potential conflicts of interest | Failing to implement conflict-checking systems, resulting in adverse representation against former clients |
| LE-3.5 | Business transaction with clients | Avoid entering into business transactions with current clients unless terms are fair, fully disclosed in writing, and the client receives independent advice | Pressuring clients into investment opportunities, loans, or business arrangements that exploit the attorney-client relationship |

## Marketing

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| LE-4.1 | Outcome representation honesty | Market legal services with accurate, non-guaranteed language about potential outcomes and past results | Making guaranteed outcome claims, success rate assertions without context, or misleading promises about case results |
| LE-4.2 | Fear-free advertising | Present legal services based on competence and value rather than exploiting fear or anxiety about legal consequences | Using fear-based advertising that exaggerates legal risks to pressure individuals into retaining counsel |
| LE-4.3 | Comparative advertising accuracy | Ensure any comparative claims about firm capabilities, results, or qualifications are verifiable and not misleading | Publishing deceptive comparative advertisements that misrepresent competitors, fabricate superiority claims, or present atypical results as typical |
| LE-4.4 | Testimonial authenticity | Use only genuine, uncoerced client testimonials with appropriate disclaimers about past results not guaranteeing future outcomes | Fabricating testimonials, coaching clients on reviews, or presenting paid endorsements as organic client experiences |

## Data

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| LE-5.1 | Post-engagement data retention | Retain client data only as long as legally required or with explicit client authorization, and securely dispose of data thereafter | Retaining client files, personal data, and case materials indefinitely after engagement ends without client consent or legal obligation |
| LE-5.2 | Breach notification timeliness | Notify clients promptly of any data breach affecting their information and provide clear remediation guidance | Failing to notify clients of data breaches involving their confidential information within a reasonable timeframe |
| LE-5.3 | Third-party vendor consent | Obtain client consent before sharing data with any third-party vendor, including cloud storage, analytics, or processing services | Sharing client data with third-party vendors—such as legal research platforms, AI tools, or data processors—without informed client consent |
| LE-5.4 | Privilege protection in data systems | Implement technical safeguards to ensure client data stored in digital systems maintains attorney-client privilege protections | Using data systems that commingle client data, weaken privilege claims, or expose confidential communications to unauthorized access |
| LE-5.5 | Client data portability | Provide clients with accessible copies of their own case files and data upon request, in a usable digital format | Refusing to provide clients with their own case data or imposing excessive fees and delays for file transfers |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this standard should:

1. **Establish Billing Integrity Protocols** — Implement time-tracking audits, rate disclosure requirements, and periodic billing reviews to ensure accuracy and fairness.
2. **Create Access-to-Justice Programs** — Develop formal pro bono policies, sliding scale fee schedules, and plain-language self-help resources as part of firm operations.
3. **Maintain Conflict-of-Interest Registers** — Maintain and regularly audit comprehensive conflict registers covering client relationships, referral arrangements, and business interests.
4. **Review Marketing Materials** — Routinely audit all advertising, website content, and outreach materials for accuracy, avoiding guarantees and fear-based messaging.
5. **Implement Data Governance Frameworks** — Establish clear data retention policies, breach response plans, and third-party vendor assessment processes aligned with client consent requirements.
6. **Train All Personnel** — Require regular ethics training covering billing practices, access commitments, conflict identification, marketing standards, and data stewardship for attorneys and support staff.
