# Sports & Athletics — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Sports & Athletics  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to professional and collegiate sports organizations, athletic governing bodies, team owners, and sports media companies. Sports occupy a privileged cultural position built on public trust, community investment, and athlete dedication. Ethical violations here exploit that trust, endanger athlete health, and commodify both competitors and fans.

---

## Category 1: Athlete Welfare

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SP-1.1 | **Brain Injury Concealment**     | Withholding or downplaying concussion diagnoses; pressuring athletes to return to play before full neurological recovery | Endangering long-term brain health for competitive advantage |
| SP-1.2 | **Scholarship One-Year Renewal Trap** | Offering athletic scholarships that are effectively year-to-year, allowing revocation for performance reasons while presenting them as four-year commitments | Exploiting student-athlete labor under false promise |
| SP-1.3 | **NIL Exploitation**            | Brokering name, image, and likeness deals that capture disproportionate value for intermediaries while athletes receive below-market compensation | Profiting from athlete brand value without fair distribution |
| SP-1.4 | **Medical Override Pressure**    | Coaching or management staff influencing medical staff to clear athletes against clinical judgment | Overriding professional medical standards for competitive gain |
| SP-1.5 | **Retirement Transition Neglect** | Failing to provide mental health, career counseling, or financial literacy support for athletes transitioning out of professional sports | Abandoning individuals after extracting peak competitive value |

## Category 2: Fan Exploitation

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SP-2.1 | **Ticket Price Escalation**      | Systematically raising ticket prices beyond inflation, supported by dynamic pricing algorithms that exploit demand spikes | Extracting monopoly rent from community loyalty |
| SP-2.2 | **Blackout Policy**              | Restricting local broadcast of sold-out games, forcing fans to purchase expensive cable packages or attend in person | Denying access to publicly supported teams |
| SP-2.3 | **Merchandise Licensing Cost**   | Charging premium prices for officially licensed fan gear while paying minimal royalties to the athletes whose names drive sales | Profiting from athlete identity without fair compensation |
| SP-2.4 | **Personal Seat License Traps**  | Requiring one-time seat licensing fees that confer no ownership rights and can be revoked or relocated | Monetizing fan loyalty through perpetual fees |
| SP-2.5 | **Relocation Threat Leveraging**  | Threatening to move teams to extract public stadium financing from host cities | Exploiting civic identity and public funds |

## Category 3: Data

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SP-3.1 | **Biometric Data Collection and Sale** | Tracking athlete heart rate, movement, sleep, and recovery metrics through wearables and selling or sharing that data without consent | Violating bodily and data autonomy |
| SP-3.2 | **Performance Data Hoarding**     | Collecting granular performance analytics on athletes while restricting their access to their own data | Information asymmetry disadvantaging athletes in contract negotiations |
| SP-3.3 | **Fan Data Monetization**         | Aggregating and selling fan purchase, attendance, and behavioral data to third parties without meaningful consent | Exploiting consumer loyalty for undisclosed data revenue |
| SP-3.4 | **Scouting Surveillance**         | Using facial recognition, drone footage, or social media monitoring to surveil amateur athletes without their awareness | Extending surveillance into youth and amateur sports |
| SP-3.5 | **Wagering Data Partnerships**    | Selling real-time performance data feeds to sportsbooks for in-game betting markets | Enabling gambling ecosystems that target athletes and fans |

## Category 4: Doping & Integrity

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SP-4.1 | **Anti-Doping Inconsistency**    | Applying testing protocols unevenly across sports, regions, or star athletes—reducing scrutiny for commercially valuable competitors | Undermining competitive fairness |
| SP-4.2 | **Match Fixing Tolerance**       | Failing to investigate or sanction suspicious officiating or performance patterns when outcomes benefit league revenue | Enabling corruption for commercial benefit |
| SP-4.3 | **Gambling Partnership Conflict** | Accepting sponsorship from sportsbooks while controlling integrity enforcement, creating inherent conflicts of interest | Institutionalizing conflict between revenue and fair play |
| SP-4.4 | **Therapeutic Use Exemption Abuse** | Approving dubious therapeutic exemptions that allow banned substance use for competitive advantage | Legalizing performance enhancement through medical loopholes |

## Category 5: Access

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SP-5.1 | **Paywall on Public Events**     | Moving publicly funded or community-rooted events behind exclusive streaming paywalls | Restricting access to cultural commons |
| SP-5.2 | **Disabled Seating Failure**     | Providing insufficient, poorly located, or overpriced accessible seating; failing to meet ADA or equivalent standards | Excluding disabled fans from participation |
| SP-5.3 | **Community Program Underfunding** | Accepting public subsidies and tax breaks while minimally funding youth athletics, recreation, or community outreach | Failing community obligations tied to public support |
| SP-5.4 | **Youth Pay-to-Play Barriers**   | Requiring expensive club fees, travel costs, and equipment that exclude low-income families from competitive pathways | Gatekeeping athletic opportunity by economic status |
| SP-5.5 | **Broadcast Regional Restriction** | Limiting game broadcasts by geography to protect legacy media deals, restricting fan access in the digital era | Prioritizing media contracts over fan access |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Adopt brain injury protocols** — enforce independent neurologist clearance for return-to-play; publish concussion incidence rates by team and season.
2. **Guarantee multi-year scholarships** — commit to four-year athletic scholarship terms that cannot be revoked for athletic performance.
3. **Disclose data practices** — publish what athlete and fan data is collected, how it is used, and with whom it is shared; obtain explicit opt-in consent for biometric data.
4. **Separate gambling and integrity** — prohibit league employees from holding sportsbook partnerships; fund independent integrity units with no commercial ties.
5. **Ensure accessible venues** — meet or exceed accessibility standards; price disabled seating at parity with comparable general admission.
6. **Invest in community access** — allocate a minimum percentage of revenue to youth programs, public recreation, and broadcast access for publicly funded venues.
