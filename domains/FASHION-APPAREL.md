# Fashion & Apparel — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Fashion & Apparel (Brands, Retailers, Fast Fashion, Luxury)  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to fashion brands, apparel retailers, fast fashion companies, and luxury houses. The fashion industry connects consumers to global supply chains where unethical practices—labor exploitation, environmental degradation, and deceptive marketing—are often hidden behind aspirational branding and glossy campaigns.

---

## Category 1: Labor & Supply Chain

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FA-1.1 | **Sweatshop Concealment**         | Failing to disclose factory locations, audit findings, or subcontracting depth, concealing labor conditions behind proprietary supply chain claims | Labor opacity; worker endangerment    |
| FA-1.2 | **Wage Theft in Supply Chain**     | Sourcing from factories that underpay workers, withhold overtime compensation, or use piece-rate schemes that result in sub-minimum wages | Economic exploitation                 |
| FA-1.3 | **Forced Labor in Cotton Sourcing** | Procuring cotton from regions with documented state-sponsored forced labor without adequate traceability or due diligence | Complicity in forced labor            |
| FA-1.4 | **Audit Theater**                 | Conducting pre-announced factory audits that allow suppliers to temporarily remediate conditions, without follow-up or worker-driven monitoring | Performative compliance               |
| FA-1.5 | **Subcontractor Omission**         | Hiding unauthorized subcontracting where workers face the worst conditions, claiming visibility only over Tier 1 suppliers | Supply chain deception               |

## Category 2: Sustainability

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FA-2.1 | **Greenwashing Fast Fashion**      | Marketing small "conscious" or "eco" collections while the vast majority of production remains environmentally destructive | Deceptive environmental claims        |
| FA-2.2 | **Deadstock Destruction**          | Incinerating or landfilling unsold inventory rather than redistributing, recycling, or marking down, to protect brand prestige | Environmental waste; anti-consumer     |
| FA-2.3 | **Microplastic Pollution Concealment** | Failing to disclose synthetic fabric shedding or opposing labeling requirements that inform consumers about microplastic release during washing | Environmental harm concealment        |
| FA-2.4 | **Renewable Material Mislabeling** | Blending minimal recycled or organic content with virgin materials while marketing the product as sustainable | Material fraud; consumer deception    |
| FA-2.5 | **Water Impact Omission**          | Advertising water reduction in finished garment processing while ignoring the far larger water footprint of raw material cultivation | Cherry-picked environmental metrics   |

## Category 3: Pricing

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FA-3.1 | **Luxury Markups Without Quality Basis** | Charging exponential price premiums driven solely by brand perception rather than material quality, craftsmanship, or ethical production | Prestige pricing exploitation         |
| FA-3.2 | **Outlet Store Deception**         | Manufacturing lower-quality goods specifically for outlet stores, implying they are excess or overstock from the main line | Deceptive value representation        |
| FA-3.3 | **Counterfeit Tolerance on Platforms** | Failing to adequately police third-party marketplace listings for counterfeits, profiting from listing fees and traffic while claiming powerlessness | Enabling intellectual property theft |
| FA-3.4 | **Perceived Scarcity Manufacturing** | Producing intentionally limited runs to create artificial scarcity and hype, driving resale prices far above retail | Artificial demand manipulation        |
| FA-3.5 | **Dynamic Pricing on Collaborations** | Applying surge pricing to limited-edition collaborations based on real-time demand rather than set retail pricing | Commodity-style price gouging         |

## Category 4: Marketing

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FA-4.1 | **Unrealistic Body Standards**     | Using digitally altered models, extreme sample sizes, or AI-generated bodies in campaigns that set unattainable appearance norms | Body image harm; deceptive representation |
| FA-4.2 | **Cultural Appropriation Without Credit** | Incorporating traditional designs, textiles, or cultural symbols from marginalized communities without attribution, compensation, or collaboration | Cultural exploitation                |
| FA-4.3 | **Influencer Undisclosed Advertising** | Paying influencers to promote products without requiring clear disclosure of the commercial relationship | Deceptive endorsement; astroturfing  |
| FA-4.4 | **Overconsumption Marketing**      | Promoting hyper-consumption through micro-seasons, trend acceleration, and "haul" culture that normalizes buying far beyond need | Environmental and financial harm      |
| FA-4.5 | **Youth-Targeted Fast Fashion**    | Marketing inexpensive, disposable fashion directly to teenagers and children using social media algorithms optimized for impulse | Exploiting vulnerable demographics    |

## Category 5: Consumer Rights

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FA-5.1 | **Return Policy Erosion**          | Shortening return windows, imposing arbitrary conditions (tags must be attached, original packaging required), or charging for returns | Undermining consumer remedy           |
| FA-5.2 | **Quality Decline Concealment**    | Reducing fabric weight, stitch count, or construction quality while maintaining or increasing prices without disclosure | Hidden quality degradation            |
| FA-5.3 | **Size Inconsistency Across Brands** | Failing to standardize sizing, enabling vanity sizing that varies by brand and prevents informed purchasing | Deceptive fit representation          |
| FA-5.4 | **Warranty & Repair Avoidance**    | Offering no repair services or warranties for garments, designing for obsolescence while claiming durability | Planned obsolescence                 |
| FA-5.5 | **Gift Card Expiration & Drift**   | Imposing expiration dates or maintenance fees on gift cards, allowing unused balances to be captured as revenue | Unjust forfeiture of consumer funds   |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Publish supply chain transparency** — disclose Tier 1 and Tier 2 factory locations, audit results, and subcontractor relationships; adopt worker-driven monitoring programs.
2. **Substantiate sustainability claims** — ensure environmental claims cover full lifecycle impact; disclose recycled content percentages; report water, carbon, and waste metrics independently verified.
3. **End deadstock destruction** — redistribute, donate, or recycle unsold inventory; publish annual deadstock volumes and disposal methods.
4. **Enforce honest marketing** — require verified influencer disclosure; prohibit digital body alteration in advertising; attribute cultural designs with compensation agreements.
5. **Standardize consumer protections** — maintain minimum 60-day return windows; disclose quality changes; adopt standardized sizing guides and publish size measurement data.
