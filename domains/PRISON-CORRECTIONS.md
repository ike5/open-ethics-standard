# Prison & Corrections — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Prison & Corrections Industry  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to private prison operators, government corrections agencies, prison service providers, and associated technology vendors. Incarcerated people are a uniquely vulnerable population unable to exit commercial relationships, making every market interaction inherently coercive. Ethical violations in this domain compound existing power imbalances and undermine rehabilitation goals.

---

## Category 1: For-Profit Exploitation

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PR-1.1 | **Inmate Communication Cost Gouging** | Charging rates for phone calls, video visits, and messaging far above market cost, often through exclusive provider contracts | Exploiting captive market; restricting family connection |
| PR-1.2 | **Commissary Price Inflation**   | Marking up essential goods—hygiene products, food, clothing—well above retail prices within facilities | Profiteering on basic necessities from a trapped population |
| PR-1.3 | **Phone Call Monopoly**           | Granting exclusive telecommunications contracts that eliminate competition and inflate per-minute rates | Enabling price-fixing in a market with no consumer choice |
| PR-1.4 | **Financial Transaction Fees**    | Imposing excessive fees on money transfers to inmates, including deposit, withdrawal, and balance inquiry charges | Extracting revenue from family support payments |
| PR-1.5 | **Visitation Technology Fees**    | Requiring paid video visitation as a substitute for free in-person visits while charging per-minute rates | Monetizing family access as a revenue center |

## Category 2: Labor

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PR-2.1 | **Forced Labor Below Minimum Wage** | Assigning incarcerated workers to jobs paying far below legal minimums, often under threat of disciplinary action | Exploiting coerced labor; undermining fair wage standards |
| PR-2.2 | **Skill Training Without Market Value** | Offering vocational programs that do not lead to recognized certifications or employment pathways | Providing false rehabilitation promise |
| PR-2.3 | **Post-Release Employment Barrier Creation** | Failing to expunge or seal records, or actively lobbying against hiring protections for formerly incarcerated people | Sustaining dependency on correctional systems |
| PR-2.4 | **Private Sector Prison Labor Contracts** | Contracting inmate labor to private corporations at below-market rates without commensurate wage pass-through | Corporate subsidy through captive labor |
| PR-2.5 | **Work Assignment Punishment**    | Using solitary confinement or loss of privileges to coerce labor participation | Forcing labor through punitive threat |

## Category 3: Healthcare

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PR-3.1 | **Inadequate Medical Care**      | Systematically understaffing medical units, delaying treatment, or denying specialist referrals | Denying fundamental healthcare rights to a captive population |
| PR-3.2 | **Mental Health Treatment Denial** | Failing to provide adequate psychiatric evaluation, counseling, or medication management | Neglecting conditions that directly affect rehabilitation and safety |
| PR-3.3 | **Medication Cost Inflation**    | Charging inmates above-market prices for prescriptions or substituting cheaper medications without clinical equivalence | Profiteering on essential healthcare |
| PR-3.4 | **Chronic Condition Neglect**     | Failing to manage ongoing conditions—diabetes, hypertension, HIV—through consistent monitoring and medication | Endangering long-term health for cost savings |
| PR-3.5 | **Reproductive Healthcare Denial** | Restricting access to prenatal care, contraception, or reproductive choice beyond legal requirements | Imposing bodily autonomy restrictions beyond sentencing |

## Category 4: Data

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PR-4.1 | **Inmate Data Monetization**    | Selling or sharing inmate data—call records, purchase histories, location data—to third parties without consent | Exploiting a population with no ability to opt out |
| PR-4.2 | **Family Location Tracking**     | Using communication metadata to surveil, locate, or profile family members outside the facility | Extending surveillance beyond the sentenced individual |
| PR-4.3 | **Biometric Collection Without Consent** | Collecting fingerprints, iris scans, voiceprints, or DNA without clear policy on storage, sharing, or deletion scope | Violating bodily and data autonomy |
| PR-4.4 | **Predictive Risk Scoring**      | Deploying algorithmic risk assessments for parole or classification based on biased or opaque data models | Automating discrimination through unaccountable systems |
| PR-4.5 | **Re-Identification of Anonymized Data** | Sharing allegedly anonymized datasets that can be cross-referenced to identify current or former inmates | Undermining privacy protections through data linkage |

## Category 5: Transparency

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PR-5.1 | **Facility Condition Concealment** | Blocking independent inspection access, classifying condition reports, or suppressing evidence of overcrowding and unsanitary conditions | Obscuring inhumane conditions from public accountability |
| PR-5.2 | **Inspection Evasion**           | Scheduling inspections after remediation efforts, providing advance notice that enables temporary compliance | Undermining oversight processes |
| PR-5.3 | **Incident Reporting Suppression** | Failing to report assaults, deaths, or self-harm incidents; classifying events to avoid triggering mandatory reviews | Concealing violence and neglect |
| PR-5.4 | **Contract Opacity**             | Shielding private prison contracts, per-diem rates, and performance metrics from public disclosure | Preventing taxpayer and civic oversight of publicly funded institutions |
| PR-5.5 | **Recidivism Data Manipulation** | Reporting selective recidivism metrics or redefining measurement windows to present favorable outcomes | Misrepresenting rehabilitation effectiveness |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Cap communication costs** — ensure phone, video, and messaging rates do not exceed regulated benchmarks; publish all fee schedules publicly.
2. **Pay fair wages** — provide incarcerated workers wages at or above applicable minimums, with transparent deductions and account access.
3. **Guarantee healthcare access** — meet community-standard healthcare benchmarks; publish staffing ratios and specialist availability.
4. **Restrict data practices** — prohibit sale of inmate data; obtain explicit consent for biometric collection; publish data retention and deletion policies.
5. **Open facility records** — permit unannounced independent inspections; publish incident reports, population counts, and condition assessments quarterly.
6. **Eliminate monopoly pricing** — allow competitive bidding for commissary and communication contracts; ban exclusive dealing arrangements that inflate costs.
