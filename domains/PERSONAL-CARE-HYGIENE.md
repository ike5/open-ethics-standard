# Domain: Personal Care & Hygiene

**Version:** 2026.0.1
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This standard defines ethical practices and violations within the personal care and hygiene products industry, covering ingredient safety, marketing honesty, environmental responsibility, product accessibility, and labeling integrity. It addresses practices that conceal health risks, exploit insecurities, degrade the environment, restrict access to essential products, or mislead consumers about product attributes. Organizations operating under this domain standard commit to safe, honest, accessible, and environmentally responsible personal care products.

---

## Ingredient Safety

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| PC-1.1 | Endocrine disruptor disclosure | Test products for endocrine-disrupting chemicals and disclose their presence with clear health risk information | Concealing known endocrine disruptors—such as parabens, phthalates, and triclosan—in product formulations or failing to warn consumers of associated health risks |
| PC-1.2 | Carcinogen elimination | Work toward eliminating known and probable carcinogens from product formulations and disclose any remaining presence | Formulating products with known or probable carcinogens—such as formaldehyde releasers, 1,4-dioxane, or coal tar—without disclosure or efforts to find safer alternatives |
| PC-1.3 | Allergen transparency | Clearly disclose all known allergens, sensitizers, and irritants in product ingredient lists using standardized naming conventions | Under-disclosing allergens, using obscure chemical names that obscure known sensitizers, or failing to flag common allergens in fragrance compounds |
| PC-1.4 | Ingredient list completeness | Disclose all ingredients, including those in fragrance, color, and preservative systems, without trade secret exemptions that hide health-relevant information | Using fragrance or trade secret exemptions to conceal health-relevant ingredients, preventing consumers from making informed safety decisions |
| PC-1.5 | Safety testing rigor | Conduct and disclose comprehensive safety testing, including long-term exposure studies, before market introduction | Marketing products with inadequate safety testing, relying on outdated or insufficient studies, or withholding unfavorable safety data |

## Marketing

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| PC-2.1 | Health-based advertising honesty | Market products based on verified benefits without exaggerating health risks of not using the product | Using fear-based hygiene marketing that exaggerates health risks—implying ordinary conditions cause serious illness—to drive product purchases |
| PC-2.2 | Body-positive representation | Represent diverse body types, skin tones, and appearances in marketing without implying any are inherently flawed | Employing body shaming in advertising—implying natural body odor, skin texture, or appearance is shameful and requires correction |
| PC-2.3 | Gender-neutral pricing | Price identical or substantially similar products equally regardless of gendered marketing or packaging | Applying gendered pricing—charging more for products marketed to women when the formulation and function are substantially identical to men's versions |
| PC-2.4 | Claims substantiation | Substantiate all product performance claims with verifiable evidence from appropriate testing methodologies | Making unsubstantiated performance claims—anti-aging, clinical results, before-and-after comparisons—without rigorous, peer-reviewed, or independently verified evidence |
| PC-2.5 | Inclusive representation | Feature models and testimonials representative of the full range of the product's intended user demographics | Exclusively featuring narrow beauty standards in marketing, implying that only certain body types, skin tones, or ages represent desirable outcomes |

## Environmental

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| PC-3.1 | Microplastic elimination | Eliminate microplastics and persistent synthetic exfoliants from all rinse-off products and disclose microplastic content in leave-on products | Replacing banned microbeads with alternative persistent synthetic particles that pose equivalent environmental harm without addressing the root problem |
| PC-3.2 | Packaging sustainability | Develop and implement packaging reduction targets, transition to recyclable or refillable systems, and disclose packaging waste data | Maintaining reliance on single-use plastic packaging with no reduction targets, refill alternatives, or sustainability commitments |
| PC-3.3 | PFAS elimination | Test products for per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, eliminate intentional PFAS use, and disclose any residual PFAS content | Formulating products with PFAS—persistent forever chemicals—without disclosure, or claiming PFAS-free status while allowing trace contamination above safe thresholds |
| PC-3.4 | Water stewardship | Measure, disclose, and reduce water usage in product manufacturing and formulate waterless alternatives where viable | Extracting water resources for product manufacturing without measuring, disclosing, or reducing water consumption impact |
| PC-3.5 | Ingredient biodegradability | Prioritize biodegradable ingredients in rinse-off products and disclose biodegradability data for all surfactants and preservatives | Using persistent, non-biodegradable ingredients in rinse-off products that accumulate in waterways and aquatic ecosystems |

## Access

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| PC-4.1 | Essential product pricing fairness | Price essential hygiene products—soap, toothpaste, menstrual products—at accessible levels and offer affordable generic alternatives | Inflating prices on essential hygiene products beyond reasonable margins, particularly during public health crises or in underserved communities |
| PC-4.2 | Distribution equity | Ensure product availability across geographic and socioeconomic markets, including low-income and rural communities | Manipulating product availability—restricting affordable product lines to premium stores while selling only expensive versions in low-income areas |
| PC-4.3 | Size-inclusive product design | Design products in sizes and formats that accommodate diverse body sizes, shapes, and mobility needs | Designing products that only work for narrow size ranges, effectively excluding larger or smaller body types from effective use |
| PC-4.4 | Accessibility compliance | Ensure packaging, instructions, and product design accommodate users with visual, manual, or cognitive disabilities | Designing packaging with child-resistant mechanisms that also exclude elderly or arthritic users, or failing to provide accessible labeling for visually impaired consumers |

## Labeling

| Ref | Practice | Description | Ethical Violation |
|-----|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| PC-5.1 | Environmental claim accuracy | Substantiate all environmental claims—biodegradable, recyclable, sustainable—with specific, verifiable evidence and clear scope definitions | Greenwashing through vague environmental claims—eco-friendly, planet-safe, green—without specific, verifiable supporting evidence |
| PC-5.2 | Cruelty-free verification | Use cruelty-free claims only when verified by an independent, accredited certification body with clear methodology and audit standards | Misrepresenting products as cruelty-free without independent verification, or using loopholes—selling in markets requiring animal testing while claiming cruelty-free status elsewhere |
| PC-5.3 | Natural and organic claim standards | Use natural and organic claims only when products meet defined, verifiable standards with ingredient-level certification | Making natural claims without standards—labeling products natural when they contain synthetic ingredients, or using organic claims without certification |
| PC-5.4 | Ingredient source transparency | Disclose key ingredient sourcing origins, particularly for ingredients associated with environmental or labor risks | Failing to disclose sourcing for high-risk ingredients—palm oil, mica, shea butter—obscuring supply chain labor and environmental conditions |
| PC-5.5 | Expiration and shelf-life clarity | Clearly mark expiration dates and period-after-opening indicators on all products with safety-relevant degradation | Omitting or obscuring expiration dates, using only production codes legible to manufacturers, or extending shelf-life claims beyond safety evidence |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this standard should:

1. **Conduct Ingredient Safety Audits** — Review all product formulations against current endocrine disruptor, carcinogen, and allergen databases, and publish findings.
2. **Review Marketing Materials** — Audit all advertising and packaging for fear-based messaging, body shaming, gendered pricing, and unsubstantiated claims.
3. **Set Environmental Targets** — Establish measurable targets for packaging reduction, PFAS elimination, microplastic removal, and water stewardship with annual public progress reporting.
4. **Ensure Access and Inclusivity** — Evaluate product pricing, distribution, size ranges, and accessibility compliance, and address gaps in underserved communities.
5. **Standardize Labeling Practices** — Adopt clear labeling policies for environmental claims, cruelty-free status, natural and organic claims, ingredient sourcing, and expiration dates.
6. **Train Product and Marketing Teams** — Require regular ethics training covering ingredient safety, responsible marketing, environmental impact, access equity, and labeling integrity.
