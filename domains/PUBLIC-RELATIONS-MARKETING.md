# Public Relations & Marketing — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Public Relations & Marketing  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to public relations agencies, marketing firms, communications consultants, and brand strategy companies. PR and marketing professionals shape public perception at scale; unethical practices in this domain erode societal trust, distort democratic discourse, and enable harm across every industry they serve.

---

## Category 1: Truth & Accuracy

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PU-1.1 | **Astroturfing**                 | Creating fake grassroots campaigns, front groups, or paid "independent" voices to simulate public support | Manufacturing consent; deceiving the public |
| PU-1.2 | **Fake Review Generation**       | Coordinating or purchasing fabricated reviews, testimonials, or endorsements to inflate reputation | Systematic deception of consumers |
| PU-1.3 | **Crisis Cover-Up Strategy**     | Designing communications strategies to minimize, deflect, or bury legitimate public health or safety concerns | Obstructing public interest information |
| PU-1.4 | **Statistics Manipulation**      | Selectively citing, misrepresenting, or recontextualizing data to support a predetermined narrative | Misleading through distorted evidence |
| PU-1.5 | **Source Concealment**           | Failing to disclose paid placement, sponsored content, or client relationships in published materials | Violating transparency in public communications |

## Category 2: Client Conflicts

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PU-2.1 | **Simultaneous Representation of Opposing Interests** | Representing clients whose interests directly conflict—e.g., a polluter and an environmental group—without disclosure | Conflict of interest undermining fiduciary duty |
| PU-2.2 | **Revolving Door with Regulators** | Hiring former regulators to lobby their former agencies or leveraging government relationships for client access | Institutional corruption; regulatory capture |
| PU-2.3 | **NDAs Hiding Public Harm**      | Using non-disclosure agreements to suppress information about environmental, health, or safety risks | Concealing information the public has a right to know |
| PU-2.4 | **Conflict Concealment in Pitches** | Withdrawing from competitive pitches after gaining confidential strategy information from a prospective client | Misappropriating competitive intelligence |

## Category 3: Data

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PU-3.1 | **Audience Profiling Without Consent** | Building detailed psychographic or behavioral profiles from social media, browsing, or purchase data without informed consent | Privacy violation; manipulation enablement |
| PU-3.2 | **Dark Ad Placement**            | Running targeted political or issue advertisements visible only to specific demographics with no public record | Undermining democratic transparency |
| PU-3.3 | **Psychographic Targeting for Manipulation** | Using personality modeling to deliver messages designed to exploit individual psychological vulnerabilities | Weaponizing psychology for persuasion |
| PU-3.4 | **Data Retention Beyond Purpose** | Retaining campaign audience data indefinitely after campaigns end, repurposing it for unrelated clients | Exceeding scope of original consent |
| PU-3.5 | **Cross-Client Data Sharing**    | Sharing audience insights, segmentation data, or proprietary research between clients without authorization | Breaching client confidentiality; competitive unfairness |

## Category 4: Labor

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PU-4.1 | **Unpaid Internship Abuse**      | Using intern programs for free labor on billable client work without meaningful mentorship or career pathways | Exploiting aspiring professionals |
| PU-4.2 | **Pitch Work Without Compensation** | Requiring extensive creative or strategic work as part of competitive pitches without compensating finalists | Extracting free intellectual labor |
| PU-4.3 | **Burnout Culture**              | Normalizing excessive hours, constant availability, and weekend work as industry expectations | Systemic harm to worker health and well-being |
| PU-4.4 | **Freelancer Payment Delays**    | Systematically delaying payment to freelance creatives, consultants, and contractors beyond contract terms | Cash flow exploitation of precarious workers |
| PU-4.5 | **Portfolio Rights Grab**         | Claiming ownership of employee or freelancer portfolio work beyond what is needed for client delivery | Restricting career mobility and creative ownership |

## Category 5: Ethics Washing

| Ref   | Practice                         | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| PU-5.1 | **Greenwashing Campaigns**       | Promoting environmental credentials that are marginal, misleading, or unsupported by actual practices | Deceiving consumers about environmental impact |
| PU-5.2 | **Purpose-Washing**              | Aligning brands with social causes through campaigns while the client's core business practices contradict those values | Co-opting social movements for commercial gain |
| PU-5.3 | **Virtue Signaling Without Action** | Launching awareness campaigns or public commitments that are not backed by measurable, time-bound operational changes | Performing ethics without substance |
| PU-5.4 | **Selective Transparency**       | Disclosing favorable sustainability or diversity metrics while withholding unfavorable ones | Cherry-picking data to manufacture an ethical image |
| PU-5.5 | **Third-Party Endorsement Laundering** | Securing endorsements, certifications, or rankings from organizations the client funds or influences | Creating false independence for paid credibility |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Disclose all paid communications** — label sponsored content, native advertising, and influencer partnerships clearly; prohibit undisclosed paid placement.
2. **Implement conflict-of-interest protocols** — maintain a client registry; decline or walls-off engagements where interests directly conflict; disclose revolving-door hires.
3. **Adopt data ethics standards** — obtain informed consent before audience profiling; delete campaign data within 12 months of campaign conclusion; prohibit cross-client data sharing without authorization.
4. **Ensure fair labor practices** — pay interns at least local minimum wage; compensate pitch finalists for substantive creative work; enforce maximum weekly hour policies.
5. **Verify sustainability and purpose claims** — require third-party verification for environmental and social impact claims; publish verification methodology alongside campaigns.
6. **Prohibit ethics-washing review** — conduct annual audits of client campaigns against actual operational practices; refuse engagements where claims cannot be substantiated.
