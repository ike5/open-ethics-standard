# Mental Health — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Mental Health Services & Platforms  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to mental health service providers, digital therapy platforms, crisis lines, telehealth companies, and psychiatric medication prescribers. Mental health care involves humanity's most vulnerable data, delivered during moments of acute distress. Unethical practices in this domain—data exploitation, access barriers, quality degradation, and medication mismanagement—compound the very suffering these services exist to alleviate.

---

## Category 1: Access & Affordability

| Ref   | Practice                              | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MH-1.1 | **Insurance Coverage Denial for Mental Health** | Systematically denying mental health claims through utilization review, prior authorization burden, or narrower networks than physical health | Parity law violation; systemic access denial |
| MH-1.2 | **Sliding Scale Avoidance**          | Offering services only at full fee while marketing community commitment, avoiding sliding scale or pro bono arrangements | Access barrier for low-income populations |
| MH-1.3 | **Session Limit Enforcement**         | Imposing arbitrary session caps that interrupt therapeutic progress without clinical justification | Prioritizing cost control over patient welfare |
| MH-1.4 | **Out-of-Network Barrier Creation**  | Maintaining intentionally inadequate provider networks that force patients into out-of-network care with higher costs | Manufactured financial barrier; parity violation |

## Category 2: Data Privacy

| Ref   | Practice                              | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MH-2.1 | **Therapy Data Sale to Advertisers** | Selling or sharing therapy session content, session metadata, or therapeutic progress data with advertising platforms | Exploitation of vulnerable patient data; trust violation |
| MH-2.2 | **Crisis Line Data Retention**        | Retaining caller data, transcripts, or metadata from crisis hotlines beyond the immediate crisis for analytics or profiling | Exploiting distress data; consent violation during vulnerability |
| MH-2.3 | **AI Chatbot Conversation Harvesting** | Using conversations from AI mental health chatbots for training, product improvement, or data sale without explicit, informed consent | Covert data extraction from help-seeking individuals |
| MH-2.4 | **Insurance Data Breach Enabling Discrimination** | Sharing diagnostic or treatment data with insurers in ways that enable coverage denial, premium increases, or employment discrimination | Enabling downstream harm from sensitive health data |

## Category 3: Quality

| Ref   | Practice                              | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MH-3.1 | **Therapist Credential Opacity**     | Failing to verify or transparently display provider credentials, training, or specialization | Enabling unqualified care; consumer deception |
| MH-3.2 | **AI Therapy Without Clinical Oversight** | Deploying AI-driven therapy tools without licensed clinician review, escalation protocols, or safety guardrails | Unaccountable automation in life-critical care |
| MH-3.3 | **Waitlist Exploitation for Premium Tiers** | Creating artificially long waitlists for standard service while offering immediate access to premium-paying clients | Exploiting distress for upselling; access inequality |
| MH-3.4 | **Crisis Response Failure**          | Failing to maintain adequate crisis protocols, response times, or escalation pathways for patients expressing suicidal ideation | Patient endangerment; negligence       |

## Category 4: Platform Ethics

| Ref   | Practice                              | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MH-4.1 | **Matching Algorithm Bias**          | Using therapist-matching algorithms that systematically steer marginalized patients to less experienced or lower-rated providers | Discriminatory care allocation         |
| MH-4.2 | **Subscription Dark Patterns During Crisis** | Using difficult cancellation flows, retention prompts, or emotional messaging to prevent churn when users are in acute distress | Exploiting vulnerability; consent manipulation |
| MH-4.3 | **Review Manipulation**              | Suppressing negative reviews, incentivizing positive reviews, or preventing patients from sharing treatment experiences | Consumer deception; accountability avoidance |
| MH-4.4 | **Therapist Non-Compete Enforcement** | Enforcing non-compete clauses that prevent therapists from continuing care with patients after leaving a platform | Interrupting therapeutic relationships; patient abandonment |

## Category 5: Medication

| Ref   | Practice                              | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| MH-5.1 | **Psychiatric Medication Overprescription** | Prescribing psychotropic medication without adequate evaluation, therapy-first approaches, or regular reassessment of continued need | Chemical intervention without clinical justification |
| MH-5.2 | **Black Box Warning Minimization**   | Downplaying or failing to adequately communicate FDA black box warnings about suicide risk, dependency, or severe side effects | Withholding life-critical safety information |
| MH-5.3 | **Sample-Driven Prescribing**         | Starting patients on medication based on pharmaceutical sample availability rather than clinical appropriateness, creating path dependency | Pharmaceutical influence on clinical judgment |
| MH-5.4 | **Telehealth Stimulant Diversion**    | Prescribing controlled stimulants via telehealth without adequate evaluation, verification, or follow-up monitoring | Enabling controlled substance diversion; regulatory violation |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Enforce parity in practice** — maintain mental health provider networks equivalent in size and access to physical health networks; eliminate unnecessary prior authorization for mental health services.
2. **Protect patient data absolutely** — never sell or share therapy data with advertisers; retain crisis line data only for immediate safety needs; obtain explicit, separate consent for any secondary data use.
3. **Ensure clinical quality** — verify and display all provider credentials; maintain licensed clinician oversight for AI tools; publish average wait times and match patients by clinical need, not payment tier.
4. **Implement safe medication practices** — require comprehensive evaluation before initial psychotropic prescriptions; communicate all black box warnings in accessible language; monitor telehealth prescriptions for diversion indicators.
5. **Eliminate dark patterns** — make subscription cancellation as easy as enrollment; never use retention tactics targeting users in crisis; allow therapists to continue patient relationships post-departure.
6. **Maintain crisis readiness** — staff crisis lines to meet demand; publish response time metrics; ensure AI tools escalate to licensed clinicians when risk indicators appear.
