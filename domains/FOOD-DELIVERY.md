# Food Delivery Platforms — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Food Delivery Platforms (Online Food Ordering, Restaurant Delivery Marketplaces)  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to food delivery platforms that connect consumers, restaurants, and delivery workers. These platforms wield enormous influence over local economies, labor conditions, and consumer welfare; unethical practices—tip theft, algorithmic wage manipulation, predatory restaurant commissions, and consumer fee deception—exploit every participant in the delivery chain.

---

## Category 1: Worker Exploitation

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FE-1.1 | **Tip Appropriation**             | Redirecting customer tips toward base pay or platform fees instead of delivering them fully to workers, obscuring tip allocation in the checkout flow | Wage theft; deception of consumers and workers |
| FE-1.2 | **Algorithmic Wage Manipulation** | Using opaque algorithms to adjust per-delivery pay based on predicted worker acceptance thresholds, systematically suppressing earnings below minimum wage | Algorithmic exploitation; wage suppression |
| FE-1.3 | **No Minimum Earnings Guarantee** | Offering no guaranteed hourly minimum, leaving workers vulnerable to earnings collapse during low-demand periods or unfavorable batch assignments | Economic precarity; labor denial |
| FE-1.4 | **Deactivation Without Appeal**   | Deactivating worker accounts based on automated metrics—acceptance rate, customer ratings—without human review, transparent explanation, or meaningful appeal process | Due process violation; algorithmic firing |
| FE-1.5 | **Multi-Platform Penalty**        | Penalizing or deactivating workers who work for competing platforms, restricting their ability to earn a living across multiple services | Anti-competitive labor restriction |

## Category 2: Restaurant Impact

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FE-2.1 | **Commission Rate Escalation**    | Progressively increasing commission percentages—often exceeding 30%—without proportional service or visibility improvements, squeezing thin restaurant margins | Rent extraction; monopolistic abuse  |
| FE-2.2 | **Search Ranking Pay-to-Play**    | Requiring restaurants to purchase advertising or accept unfavorable terms to achieve organic search visibility, blurring the line between ads and results | Monetizing fair access; deceptive ranking |
| FE-2.3 | **Data-Driven Competing Ghost Kitchens** | Analyzing restaurant order data—popular items, demand patterns, pricing—to launch competing ghost kitchens that replicate successful menu concepts | Intellectual property appropriation; unfair competition |
| FE-2.4 | **Forced Participation in Promotions** | Enrolling restaurants in discount promotions or free-delivery campaigns without consent, absorbing the cost from restaurant margins while the platform claims marketing credit | Coercive commercial terms; cost shifting |
| FE-2.5 | **Contractual Unilateral Changes** | Modifying commission rates, service areas, or agreement terms unilaterally with minimal notice, forcing take-it-or-leave-it acceptance | Unequal bargaining power              |

## Category 3: Consumer Practices

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FE-3.1 | **Service Fee Stacking**          | Layering multiple fees—service fee, delivery fee, small-order fee, regulatory response fee—at checkout, making the true cost of ordering opaque | Bait-and-add; cost concealment        |
| FE-3.2 | **Delivery Radius Manipulation**  | Artificially extending delivery radii beyond practical travel time, resulting in cold food and degraded quality while collecting full fees | Misrepresentation; quality deception  |
| FE-3.3 | **Refund Difficulty for Order Errors** | Making refund requests for missing items, wrong orders, or late deliveries arduous through chatbot loops, partial credit-only refunds, or requiring photo evidence for obvious errors | Undermining consumer remedy            |
| FE-3.4 | **Surge Pricing During Weather Events** | Implementing dynamic delivery fee surges during storms, extreme heat, or emergencies—precisely when consumers are most reliant on delivery | Price gouging on necessity; exploiting vulnerability |
| FE-3.5 | **Menu Price Inflation Without Disclosure** | Allowing or encouraging menu prices on the platform to exceed in-store prices without clearly disclosing the markup | Hidden cost inflation; price deception |

## Category 4: Data

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FE-4.1 | **Restaurant Customer Data Hoarding** | Preventing restaurants from accessing customer data for orders placed through the platform, severing the restaurant-customer relationship | Data hostage-taking; relationship appropriation |
| FE-4.2 | **Delivery Location Tracking Beyond Order** | Continuing GPS tracking of delivery workers beyond active deliveries, or tracking consumer location after order completion | Surveillance overreach; location privacy violation |
| FE-4.3 | **Algorithmic Demand Prediction for Platform Benefit** | Using aggregate restaurant and consumer data to predict demand patterns, then leveraging that intelligence for ghost kitchen expansion or ad pricing—benefiting the platform at the expense of data-originating restaurants | Data extraction; conflicts of interest |
| FE-4.4 | **Cross-App Data Sharing**        | Sharing consumer ordering data with affiliated services or selling to data brokers without clear, affirmative consent | Privacy violation; data trafficking   |

---

## Category 5: Safety

| Ref   | Practice                          | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| FE-5.1 | **Pressure to Speed Encouraging Unsafe Driving** | Setting unrealistic delivery time estimates and penalizing late arrivals, incentivizing workers to speed, run red lights, or ride recklessly | Endangering workers and public safety |
| FE-5.2 | **No Coverage for On-the-Job Injuries** | Classifying delivery workers as independent contractors to avoid providing workers' compensation for injuries sustained during active deliveries | Labor classification abuse; injury abandonment |
| FE-5.3 | **Inadequate Insurance for Accidents During Delivery** | Providing minimal or nonexistent accident insurance for workers while on the road, leaving them financially exposed for vehicle damage or third-party liability | Insurance negligence; risk shifting    |
| FE-5.4 | **Food Safety Oversight Gaps**    | Failing to ensure delivery workers maintain food-safe temperatures or hygienic conditions, with no verification mechanisms for transit integrity | Public health negligence              |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Protect worker earnings** — deliver 100% of customer tips to workers, provide transparent per-delivery pay breakdowns, and guarantee a minimum hourly earnings floor equivalent to local minimum wage.
2. **Ensure fair restaurant terms** — cap commission rates, provide 60-day notice for contractual changes, and never use restaurant data to launch competing offerings.
3. **Make consumer costs transparent** — display all fees before checkout, itemize each charge separately, and disclose any menu price markup relative to in-store pricing.
4. **Respect data rights** — provide restaurants with their own customer order data upon request, cease location tracking when deliveries end, and obtain affirmative consent before cross-app data sharing.
5. **Prioritize safety** — set realistic delivery time expectations that do not incentivize speeding, provide occupational accident coverage for active delivery workers, and implement food-safe transit verification.
