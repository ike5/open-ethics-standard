# Shipping & Freight — Domain-Specific Ethics Standards

**Domain:** Shipping & Freight (Carriers, Freight Forwarders, Logistics Platforms, Last-Mile Delivery)  
**Version:** 2026.0.1  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## Purpose

This document extends the Open Ethics Standard (OES) to shipping and freight companies, logistics platforms, and carriers. Shipping is the backbone of global commerce, and unethical practices in this domain exploit pricing opacity, worker vulnerability, environmental externalities, and data asymmetry to shift costs onto workers, consumers, and communities.

---

## Category 1: Pricing

| Ref   | Practice                                      | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SF-1.1 | **Dimensional Weight Gaming**                | Applying dimensional weight pricing rules inconsistently or using packaging dimensions that inflate billable weight | Overcharging by manipulating measurement standards |
| SF-1.2 | **Fuel Surcharge Opacity**                   | Adding fuel surcharges that do not correspond to actual fuel cost fluctuations or that persist after fuel prices decline | Using surcharges as hidden profit centers rather than cost pass-throughs |
| SF-1.3 | **Accessorial Fee Stacking**                 | Layering numerous ancillary fees (residential, liftgate, inside delivery, notification) that are not disclosed at the point of quote | Concealing the true cost of shipment through fragmented surcharges |
| SF-1.4 | **Rate Manipulation During Peak Periods**     | Implementing excessive peak-season surcharges without transparent justification tied to actual capacity constraints | Exploiting seasonal demand without proportional cost basis |
| SF-1.5 | **Minimum Charge Abuse**                     | Setting minimum shipment charges that far exceed the cost of service for lightweight or short-distance packages | Overcharging small shippers and consumers on low-cost shipments |

## Category 2: Labor

| Ref   | Practice                                      | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SF-2.1 | **Driver Detention Time Unpaid**             | Failing to compensate drivers for extended wait times at shipping and receiving facilities beyond reasonable grace periods | Extracting unpaid labor from drivers held at facilities beyond their control |
| SF-2.2 | **Warehouse Quota Abuse**                    | Setting pick, pack, or sort rates at levels that compromise worker safety, require off-the-clock work, or result in disciplinary action for natural variation | Imposing productivity standards that sacrifice worker health for throughput |
| SF-2.3 | **Misclassification of Drivers**             | Classifying truck drivers as independent contractors while exercising control over routes, schedules, and equipment | Denying drivers employment protections and benefits they legally deserve |
| SF-2.4 | **Forced Dispatch and Coerced Driving**      | Pressuring drivers to operate beyond legal hours-of-service limits or in unsafe conditions through dispatch practices | Endangering public safety and driver health to meet delivery commitments |
| SF-2.5 | **Inadequate Rest Facility Provision**       | Failing to provide safe, sanitary rest areas for drivers at terminals and distribution centers | Neglecting basic worker welfare during mandatory rest periods |

## Category 3: Environmental

| Ref   | Practice                                      | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SF-3.1 | **Emission Underreporting**                  | Understating Scope 1 and Scope 3 emissions through selective measurement, offset double-counting, or excluded fleet segments | Misleading regulators, investors, and the public about climate impact |
| SF-3.2 | **Packaging Waste Facilitation**             | Providing no incentive or infrastructure for shippers to reduce packaging, or defaulting to oversized packaging | Actively enabling excessive packaging waste through service design |
| SF-3.3 | **Return Logistics Disposal**                 | Routing returned goods to landfill or incineration rather than resale, donation, or recycling when reuse is feasible | Destroying usable products for logistical convenience |
| SF-3.4 | **Last-Mile Efficiency Neglect**             | Failing to optimize last-mile routing, consolidate shipments, or offer pickup-point alternatives that reduce per-package emissions | Accepting avoidable emissions from unoptimized delivery practices |
| SF-3.5 | **Carbon Offset Program Greenwashing**        | Marketing carbon-neutral shipping options that rely on low-quality offsets or inflated baseline calculations | Misleading consumers about the environmental impact of their shipments |

## Category 4: Data

| Ref   | Practice                                      | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SF-4.1 | **Shipment Data Monetization**               | Selling aggregated shipping volumes, trade lane data, or commodity flow information to third parties without shipper consent | Monetizing proprietary business intelligence gleaned from client transactions |
| SF-4.2 | **Tracking Data Sale**                        | Selling real-time or historical package tracking data to data brokers, marketers, or analytics firms | Exposing sender and recipient movement patterns without consent |
| SF-4.3 | **Customs Data Exploitation**                 | Leveraging customs declarations, HS code data, or trade compliance filings for competitive intelligence or resale | Exploiting mandatory regulatory submissions for commercial gain |
| SF-4.4 | **Recipient Profile Building**               | Creating consumer profiles based on shipment frequency, sender identity, and package characteristics for advertising or resale | Building personal profiles from logistics data that recipients never consented to share |
| SF-4.5 | **IoT Sensor Data Collection**               | Gathering environmental data (temperature, humidity, shock) from shipments and retaining it beyond the delivery purpose without disclosure | Hoarding sensor data that could reveal proprietary handling or storage conditions |

---

## Category 5: Consumer Rights

| Ref   | Practice                                      | Description                                                        | Ethical Violation                     |
|-------|-----------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| SF-5.1 | **Lost Package Accountability Gaps**          | Creating claim processes with short filing windows, burdensome proof requirements, or liability caps that leave consumers uncompensated | Systematically avoiding responsibility for lost or misdelivered items |
| SF-5.2 | **Insurance Mandate on Low-Value Shipments**  | Requiring or heavily pushing declared-value coverage on shipments where the risk of loss is minimal relative to the premium | Profiting from unnecessary insurance by exploiting fear of loss |
| SF-5.3 | **Delivery Window Manipulation**             | Advertising narrow delivery windows to drive sales while routinely missing them without consequence | Setting expectations to win business that cannot be met in practice |
| SF-5.4 | **Proof of Delivery Fabrication**            | Generating delivery confirmations (photo, signature, scan) that do not correspond to actual delivery to the intended recipient | Falsifying records to close shipments and avoid accountability |
| SF-5.5 | **Undisclosed Third-Party Carrier Substitution** | Handing shipments to unannounced subcontractors with different service standards, liability terms, or delivery practices | Depriving consumers of the carrier choice and service level they paid for |

## Compliance Guidelines

Organizations adopting this domain standard shall:

1. **Practice pricing transparency** — disclose all surcharges at quote, tie fuel surcharges to published indices, and apply dimensional weight rules consistently.
2. **Uphold labor standards** — compensate driver detention time, maintain safe warehouse quotas, classify workers correctly, and respect hours-of-service regulations.
3. **Reduce environmental impact** — accurately report emissions, minimize packaging waste, route returns to reuse rather than disposal, and ensure carbon offset integrity.
4. **Protect shipper and recipient data** — prohibit the sale of shipment and tracking data, restrict customs data use to compliance purposes, and destroy sensor data after delivery.
5. **Honor consumer rights** — simplify claim processes, eliminate unnecessary insurance pressure, meet advertised delivery windows, and maintain delivery proof integrity.
